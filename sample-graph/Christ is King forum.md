- Moderator guidelines
  collapsed:: true
	- 1
	  collapsed:: true
		- Our warning system is based on points. The more points you get, the longer your suspension. If you get 10 points, the software automatically bans you, though if you're about to give someone a warning that causes them to be banned, it's better to just issue the ban instead of the warning.
		- 2 points = no suspension
		  4 points = 3 day suspension
		  6 points = 7 day suspension
		  8 points = 14 day suspension
		- A lot of members don't want to read or follow the rules, so we use a punitive system that gives them an opportunity to reform their behavior before permanently banning them. Most violations you see on the forum will be small enough that they warrant a warning, not a permanent ban.
		- Typical Violations
		- Here are the most common rule violations, from least severe to most severe:
		  Textspeak - When a user doesn't use proper capitalization and punctuation. Many users post from mobile devices and while the occasional lowercase post is fine, I warn a member if they show a repeated pattern of it. I usually give 2 points for this but sometimes 4 or 6 if it's a newbie who is also flooding the forum with low quality posts.
		  Excessive profanity - We have a zero tolerance position on profanity, even if it is concealed with an asterisk. This is usually a 2 point warning.
		  Flooding the forum - Some newbies get too excited and post a lot of junk after they sign up. The forum software now throttles their posts to one every 10 minutes so this should be less of a problem. This warning attempts to slow them down. Assign 4-6 points.
		  Discussing politics/race before 25 posts - A common problem. Some users sign up only to talk about politics and race. 4-6 points.
		  Sexual content - This is no longer a PUA forum, so users cannot glorify, promote, or brag about casual sex. They also cannot ask how to get casual sex. 4-6 points.
		  Personal insult - Users can disagree with other members and have mild debates, but they cannot personally attack them or name-call. Senior members get a small pass if they make fun of a newbie who is not posting value, because he is correcting the newbie, but at the same time, senior members should not act as if they have mod authority. 2-8 points.
		  Aggressive / argumentative - Many newbies sign up and immediately start getting into arguments. They start draining the energy of the forum and increase the drama. While it's okay to argue from time to time, newbies are expected to check their pride at the door until they get settled in. 4-8 points.
		  Self-promotion - As discussed in the spammer thread, I'll warn someone who is self-promoting their own site, but if they persist, they will be treated like a lowly spammer. 6 points.
		- How Many Points To Give?
		- We want all members to be productive, to gain value from the forum and to give back. The warning system is meant to take someone who is rough around the edges, or a bit hard-headed, and make them a good fit. This means that all men must check their pride at the door before posting here (including myself). Ask yourself what length of warning will help a member correct their behavior and be a better community member. If a warning is too light, he may try to break a rule again, and if a warning is too severe, he may think he's being treated unfairly and want to leave.
		- It's better to be over lenient than overly severe. If you are overly lenient, and he breaks a rule again, then you know what type of transgressor he is. He took your mercy and ignored it, so he should not receive additional mercy. You can now throw the book at him, either sending him up to 8 points for a 2-week suspension or ban him outright.
		- Generally speaking, if a member just needs a little self-correction, give him a 2 point warning. If he's breaking the rules and needs to take time to understand his error, give him 4 points. If he's crossing the line and making it appear that he doesn't care about the community, give 6 points. If he's hanging on a thread, give 8 points or a ban.
		- You don't need to be merciful for flagrant violators. These are members who have no intention to fit in the community, and just want to cause a rampage and then leave.
		- In time, you will develop a good sense of forum justice, though we are human so our justice will always be imperfect (e.g. when you're in a bad mood, your punishments will be more severe than if you were in a good mood).
		- How To Give A Warning
		- You will come across content in violation either through your own browsing through the forum or through a report. When viewing a report, the software will first show you the reported content. Afterwards, you will see who the reporter is and why he's reporting the post. Since you will often need context of what happened, it helps to click "Go To Content" button and have that opened in a new window for a smoother workflow.
		- Step 1: View content and decide if it's in violation.
		- If it's not in violation, or is right on the edge of a violation, you can decide to take no action against the member. In that case, scroll to the bottom of the report, select "Rejected" and enter a message in the alert box telling the reporter why it's not in violation or why you decided not to take action.
		- The big box underneath Update will be for moderator eyes only. You are free to leave a message that would be helpful to the other mods. There is also a Reassign option that we may use in the future.
		- Step 2: If member is in violation, warn him by levying points.
		- Step 2a: First check how many existing points the member has by hovering over his name or visiting his profile. If member has warnings, it will be shown on the right. The first number is his points and the second number is how many warnings he has (a mnemonic to remember the order is to think that the number of points a member has is most important so it always goes first).
		- I was going to give this member 4 points, for a 1-week ban, but I see he already has 6 points. So instead I would give him a 2-point warning. His total would then increase to 8 points and he will be banned for 2-weeks. Members with existing points get higher punishments because they are in continual violation.
		- It's important to know how many existing points a member has because if you give 6 points to a member you didn't know already had 4 points, you will be banning him outright instead of giving a one-week suspension. It's okay if you make this mistake--simply revoke the warning you gave and then give a new one with less points.
		- Step 2b: Click the Warn link on the violating post or from the user's profile.
		- If you click Warn from a post, the content will be automatically placed in your warning text.
		- Step 2c: Select the warning type. Many common violations have already been pre-filled for you. If you can't find the type of warning you want to apply, select General Warning. Do not select Custom Warning, since it does not have a formatted message.
		- Step 2d: Suggested points may be pre-filled, but you are free to change it. In this case, I would hit the minus button until the points applied is 2. All warnings last for 3 months (e.g. a suspension may only be one week, but user will still have the points for 3 months).
		- Step 2e: Verify private message that goes out to warned user
		- If you select the General Warning, you have to type in a reason for the warning. For other warnings, reasons are pre-filled, though you are free to change them to be more specific. You are also free to delete the violating content that is automatically pre-filled in the message as a quote.
		- For this warning, I would type in a reason after "Reason for warning:" and change the point total to 2 underneath it. Member would see in his profile that he now has 8 points and can use the key below to know he will be banned for 14 days.
		- Step 2f: Verify remaining options and then hit Warn.
		- If the post is spam, you can use the Delete option. For high-profile warnings, you may want to post a public warning. I usually don't modify any of these options.
		- Step 2g: Go back and close initial report by selecting Approved and letting the reporter know what action you took.

		- Member disputes:
		- It's very common for two members to attack each other or get into a heated arguments. It can be difficult to apply justice in these situations since many disputes go back a long way. Here are three standards I use in coming to a fair judgement:
		- Seniority
		- Who is the senior member in the dispute? If someone has been on the forum for at least two years and has several hundred posts, I consider them a senior member. They have proved that they are a active and contributing member of the community.
		- A member of 10 years is more senior than a member of 2 years. While senior members are not immune to the law, the more senior member in a dispute gets the benefit of the doubt. This especially applies to newbies, some of whom will ceaselessly challenge the established hierarchy in order to quickly build status. We do not cater to the whims, feelings, and dictates of newbies, even if they are in the right, and do allow them to receive some minor abuse (i.e. hazing), since that hazing is usually a result of them not lurking long enough to see what type of content and analysis the community would enjoy.
		- Imagine a new parishioner to a church. Within only one month of joining, he starts complaining about the choir volume and wishes to make other changes. While he may have good intentions, he's not coming to the community with humility. If you agree to his demands, he will never stop. We consider the feelings and needs of newbies, but they haven't yet proved their worth, so we do not change or act in response to them.
		- Instigation
		- Who started it? One member is usually responsible for igniting the spark by making passive-aggressive, sarcastic, or mocking comments. Another member--or group of members--often takes the bait and start reacting to him in an aggressive manner. Ask yourself if the member who started it delivered a personal insult or was otherwise nasty to the membership. If so, he is likely to be most culpable in the dispute.
		- Members are allowed to be firm in their arguments. It's okay to point out that another member is misguided or wrong. Our line is when things get personal. That's when we get involved.
		- Proportionality
		- Lastly, how proportionate were the responses? If someone calls me stupid, and I call him stupid back, I was proportionate. But if I instead say that his mother is a harlot, I escalated the dispute and can now be warned along with the instigator.
		- We give members some leeway to defend themselves, but only in a proportionate manner. Vigilante justice is discouraged because it would lead to chaos. Therefore if senior member A calls senior member B stupid, and B responds by saying "No, you are," I would give a 4 point warning to member A and not warn B. On the other hand, if member B escalates the dispute, I would give them both warnings.
		- I resist warning senior members in their treatment of newbies, but some senior members go overboard with power. In that case, I will punish them lightly with a 2-point warning (no suspension). That is usually enough to relax their hazing.
		- There are three basic responses you can take as a mod: (1) Warn one member, (2) Warn multiple members with different punishments (usually when proportionality has been breached), or (3) Take no action. I'll choose the last option if the exchange was mild and brief.
		- When RVF was a game forum, the disputes were endless because the membership was mostly composed of prideful men deep in sin. Lately, disputes have been less common. Warnings in the 2, 4, and 6 range are most suitable for moderating them.

		- Members who respond negatively to your warning:
		- A lot of men simply can't handle receiving a warning on an internet forum. I have been amazed at how often a grown man will lose his marbles because I suspended him for three days or so. It also has happened many times where a member had a total breakdown from a warning. He responded by sending me all manner of personal insults, leaving me no choice but to apply a second warning or ban him outright.
		- Most members will not message you after you warn them. If they do, it's for one of these reasons...
		- -
		- To explain themselves or say that you're wrong. It was a mistake, you see, because the senior member who has been on the forum forever didn't treat him with respect. The member will aggressively state his case and how your decision is bad for the forum or simply not fair. Such a member can complain about your decision all he wants. Simply do not reply to him.
		- -
		- To ask for his account to be deleted. Your warning grievously offended him and now he doesn't want to be on the forum anymore. He will send you multiple messages asking how to delete his account (which the registration terms states cannot be deleted). Again, simply do not reply to him. The only valid deletion request we consider is if a member shared personal details in a post.
		- -
		- He personally offends you. A member can cry and whine about his warning, and even say you're wrong, but he cannot insult you. If he's a senior member, bring him up to 6 or 8 points. If he's a newbie, you can either do the same or outright ban him. If a newbie is already breaking the rules and insulting the mods, chances are he will not be a productive member.
		- The bottom line is to generally not reply to highly emotional messages. Nothing good will come forth because the member has lost emotional control and cannot engage in a productive dialogue. The only time I reply is to further clarify his violation (if I believe it would help him understand the rules better), answer fair and reasonable questions, or fix a mistake I made. In the latter case, I would cancel the warning and tell him I did so.
		- Whatever the case, get ready to see totally irrational displays of pride.
