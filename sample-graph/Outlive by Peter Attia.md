	- [The four big killers](https://peterattiamd.com/peter-on-the-four-horsemen-of-chronic-disease/) are cardiovascular disease, cancer, neurodegenerative disease (Alzheimer’s / dementia / Parkinson’s), and metabolic syndrome (sarcopenia, type II diabetes, being obese and then getting sick, having a cytokine storm that your body can’t fight off). Or you don’t have enough muscle mass to catch yourself from a fall.

	- Peter Attia’s model goes a bit beyond this. He wants to live to be 100, and during these last decades of his life, wants to be able to perform various life tasks that we all take for granted:
		- Bend over to pick your grandkid up
		- Get up from a sitting position on the floor (while playing with grandchildren) without using both arms
		- Take a hike
		- Carry groceries
		- Have sex
		- Etc

	- Sarcopenia is the muscle loss associated with aging. If you remain natty, some degree of muscle loss will happen. If you don’t keep training legs hard, you’ll have stick legs.
		- We want to start training extra hard today, knowing we are going to loss some of our muscle and strength.
		- Training the body is building up reserves for retirement. Eventually you’ll have to tap into your savings account. Have as much there as possible.
	- {{twitter https://twitter.com/Cernovich/status/1637912812721934336}}
		- Falls are a huge killer. Been doing lots of eyes closed one legged stuff since learning this. Since it falls with age, always build up as high a balance as possible now.
		- Inability to stand on one leg for 10 seconds linked to doubling in risk of death
		- [Inability to stand on one leg for 10 seconds linked to doubling in risk of death | BMJ](https://www.bmj.com/company/newsroom/inability-to-stand-on-one-leg-for-10-seconds-in-mid-to-later-life-linked-to-near-doubling-in-risk-of-death/)
	- The decline is real.
		- If  you do nothing, you’re going to lose 10-15% of your cardiorespiratory function as measured by VO2 max a decade. Mitochondria die off. Pounds of muscle go away and never return.
		- You end up either fat and old, or a stick figure and old. Either way, you’re one fall away from a fractured hip and limited mobility and death. Multiple diabetes 
		  medications.
		- If you train hard and smart, you can reduce the decline dramatically.
		- It’s a little frustrating to go to the gym and see the weights you put on movements decrease. Without a strong WHY for lifting, it’s easy to lose motivation. Eventually you make the transition to understanding that you’re preserving what you can.
		- No, 50 isn’t the new 30. VO2 max starts to decline in your mid-20’s, you lose fast twitch muscle fiber around that time as well. You have to really fight like hell to not fall off a cliff. You'll lose type II fibers, VO2 max and fluid intelligence / IQ
	- Did you know that a high VO2 max is associated with a lower risk of death in a way that strength training isn’t?
		- Higher VO2 max even seems to protect against cancer
		- ["VO2max, critical power and the lactate threshold become increasingly more relevant to everyday life as we age, with tasks of daily living eliciting greater percentages of VO2max as we age."](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC4808583/)

		- Muscle quality decreases over age, which limits VO2 max
		- Raising your VO2 max to an elite level lowers your risk of dying from all causes than smoking increases your risk. In other words, think about how bad a smoking habit it. Upping your VO2 is better than smoking is bad.
		- You increase VO2 max via lots of low-end aerobic activity in Zones 1 and 2, with a hard interval session every 5-10 days (depends on how quickly you recover).
		- Fast After 50 book by Joe Friel
		- Alex Hutchinson explained all of this in Endurance his book and also on Peter Attia. 99% of people doing HIIT aren't actually doing it. Read the studies yourself. HIIT isn’t one round of “Tabata.” It's 4 minutes at VO2 max, 4 minutes recovery, and repeated.
	- Blood work, heart attacks
		- Have regular blood work - esecially cholesterol, LDL, triglyceride etc
		- Getting a calcium scan on heart done. Something all men should do, if only to establish a baseline. It shows if there are any arteries clogged. Often it’s genetic. Healthy people have had sudden heart attacks for decades. Plaque builds up and you don't know until it’s too late.
		- Over the years total cholesterol crept up, despite my drinking almost no alcohol and tracking macros. Never gave it much thought as the Cholesterol Wars of decades ago suggested that high cholesterol was overstated as a risk factor for heart disease. We were told to not even track cholesterol but instead get a coronary calcium scan. Which I did, and have a score of 0, i.e., no damage to my articles showed up during this test.
		- After listening to The Peter Attia Drive #236, I got the Cardio IQ® Advanced Lipid Panel done. **Elevated Apo(b) and Apo(a).** I never would have had that lab done without the Attia podcast.
			- Attia claims a statin is the only way to bring down those values (which are genetic). I was very anti-statin due to the information circles I travel in. May have to get on one with the Apo(b) remains elevated during my next labs.
	- Now I do a few hours a week of so-called Zone 2 cardio. (That definition is hotly debated as Attia defines Zone 2 based on lactate levels, whereby one used a lactate meter to measure blood lactate levels.)
		- 1 Lactate Meter... $250. 15 lactate strips for a test.. $30
		- Others use heart rate to track Zone 2. I don’t need to stress over this stuff, and use the [MAF 180 Formula](https://philmaffetone.com/180-formula/). It’s easier to keep my heart rate in the 130 beats per minute range on an Peloton than walking. Walks are usually more Zone 1, and again, that’s fine for my purposes.
		- Lift two and a half-time a week. (It works out this way as my training is on a 10 day schedule instead of one week.)
		- When you’re in your 20’s, you lift as follows: Push, Pull, Legs, Off. Repeat.
		- In your 40’s and not on testosterone therapy, your “week” might be structured like:
			- Upper body chest + zone 2.
			- Lower body.
			- Zone 2.
			- Zone 2.
			- Intervals.
			- Off.
			- Upper body back + zone 2.
			- Upper body chest.
			- Zone 2.
			- Legs.
		- Key takeaways for exercising for health and lifespan:
			- (Each week)
			- 2-4 strength training sessions
			- 3-4 Zone 2 @ 45 minutes.
			- 1-2 true VO2 max intervals.
		- Sometimes you might have more time, then bump the Zone 2 sessions to 90 minutes or more. Other times you can only do 30 minutes. Not a big deal. Take the long view.
		- You’re not training for a bodybuilding show or contest. You’re making deposits into your life savings account.
